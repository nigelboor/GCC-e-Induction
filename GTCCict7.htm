How to choose a good password

ICT Security is everyone’s responsibility. GCC has a duty to ensure that the information it handles is kept safe. Choosing a good password is like choosing a good lock on your front door at home and this is down to each individual user to follow the basic steps below:

  • Passwords should be at least 8 characters long.
  • Utilise both lettlers and numbers, i.e. alphanumeric.
  • Use upper and lower cases.
  • Avoid using commonly known facts about yourself, e.g. name of your partner, date of birth, car registration number.
  • Try not to use words found in the dictionary. Hackers tend to use a dictionary file to crack passwords.

Just like your front door key, please do take care of your password.

Don’t write it down where it can be easily interpreted by others.

Do not give your password to anyone else – not even the Support desk staff who are trying to help!

General password advice can be found in the downloads section of this page http://staffnet.gloscc.gov.uk/index.cfm?articleid=3596 on Staffnet.

Information handling

Without information the organisation would not be able to function so please remember to take the time out to manage the council’s information properly. It could make the difference between the council making the right and wrong decision.

You must understand your responsibilities surrounding the information that you have been given access to.

Protective Marking

In order to be able to understand the level of security that is required for different types of information you should protectively mark your documents. The council holds information that falls into 3 protective marking categories:

UNCLASSIFIED

This is used to indicate positively that a protective marking is not needed. Information would fall into this category if:

  • release wouldn’t cause any damage or distress to individuals,
  • if it is freely available in the council’s publication scheme or website; or
  • no Freedom of Information (FOI) or Environmental Information Regulations (EIR) exemptions apply to the information

PROTECT

This is used to indicate that a level of security is required. Information would fall into this category if released would:

  • cause distress or potential harm to individuals; breach proper undertakings to maintain the confidence of information provided by third parties; breach statutory restrictions on the disclosure of information
  • cause financial loss or loss of earning potential, or facilitate improper gain;
  • unfair advantage for individuals or companies;
  • prejudice the investigation or facilitate the commission of crime;
  • disadvantage government in commercial or policy negotiations with others.

RESTRICTED

This is used to indicate that a high level of security is required. Information would fall into this category if released would:

  • affect diplomatic relations adversely;
  • cause substantial distress to individuals;
  • cause financial loss or loss of earning potential or to facilitate improper gain or advantage for individuals or companies;
  • prejudice the investigation or facilitate the commission of crime;
  • breach proper undertakings to maintain the confidence of information provided by third parties;
  • impede the effective development or operation of government policies;
  • breach statutory restrictions on disclosure of information;
  • disadvantage government in commercial or policy negotiations with others
  • undermine the proper management of the public sector and its operations.

Security

It is your responsibility to ensure that council information is kept secure, so please remember:

  • Lock doors and windows when leaving the office;
  • Don’t let unauthorised visitors onto Council premises;
  • Keep your password secure; don’t share it;
  • Use the screen lock whenever you leave your computer (hold down the Ctrl, Alt & Delete keys at the same time, then click on Lock Computer OR hold down the Windows key & the letter L at the same time);
  • Use confidential waste facilities to destroy information;
  • Keep information secure when not on Council premises;
  • Do not leave files in public places;
  • Do not put personal information onto unencrypted devices, e.g. laptops, memory sticks, CDs;
  • Be aware of who can hear your telephone conversations;
  • Don’t access information that you don’t need to do your job;
  • Don’t use unauthorised non-GCC equipment, e.g. home computers when using the approved remote access or personal email accounts.

Please note this list is not exhaustive and you are responsible for ensuring you keep up-to-date with the Council’s security policies.

Legislation

Within the Council we have a duty to keep information secure, but must balance that with transparency and openness. There are two key pieces of legislation that you must be aware of when using, collecting, storing, sharing, reading, or doing anything with information.

Data Protection

This is a law put in place to protect people’s information (personal data)

Personal data is any data relating to a living individual which identifies them, e.g. name, address, data of birth, national insurance number. It includes an expression of opinion about the individual and any indication of the intentions of the organisation.

Some information is more sensitive than others, e.g. information about an individual’s:

mental or physical health, political beliefs, religious beliefs, sexual life, membership of a trade union, racial or ethnic origin, criminal offences (including alleged),

There are various rules around the use of personal data, called the 8 principles.

8 Principles

  1. Fair and lawful
    Make sure people know why their information is being collected, what it will be used for and who it will be shared with.
    We must work within our powers.
    Do not use personal information for anything that will break a law.
    Must comply with the conditions in the Act, e.g. you have consent to use the personal data, or there is a legal obligation to do something.
  2. Specific purpose
    Only use the information for the reasons it was collected for.
  3. Adequate and not excessive
    Make sure you have enough information to do the task, but don’t collect information ‘just in case’.
  4. Accurate
    Make sure the information is correct.
    If it is an opinion, make sure that this is clear.
  5. Retained for as long as necessary
    Only keep information as long as needed, either by law or for business need.
    Keep information in-line with the retention schedules http://staffnet.gloscc.gov.uk/retention.
    Use the confidential facilities to destroy information.
  6. Individual’s rights
    The public and staff have the right to access the personal information held about them, including in emails.
    People have the right to claim compensation for damage and distress.
    People have the right to request use of their personal data to cease.
    People have the right to stop the use of their personal data for marketing purposes.
  7. Security
    Keep personal information secure at all times, whether electronic or manual.
    Make sure Data Protection is in agreements with contractors.
  8. Transfer out of EEA
    Be careful what you put on the Internet/website.
    Contact the Information Management Service if you need to send personal information abroad http://staffnet.gloscc.gov.uk/IMS.

Consequences of getting it wrong

  • To an individual – harm or distress, danger, identity fraud.
  • To an employee – extra work, disciplinary proceedings, dismissal, criminal prosecution, prison, fines.
  • To the Council – complaints, criminal prosecution, damaging publicity, unlimited compensation claims, fines of up to £500,000.

What is Freedom of Information (FOI)?

  • The public’s general right of access to recorded information
    – Electronic, Paper, Email, Video etc
  • Right to know what information is held
  • Right to have the information communicated to them
  • The emphasis is on disclosure
  • Put in place to find out how public money is spent and how/why decisions are taken
  • It is about improving openness, transparency and accountability in public bodies

What does this mean to us?

  • Produce and maintain a publication scheme
  • Manage our information
  • Only write things down that are accurate
  • Never write anything down that you wouldn’t want released to the public
  • Recognise requests for information
  • Assist people who are thinking of or are trying to make an FOI request
  • Respond to requests within 20 working days
  • Have a complaints/review procedure

What is a request?

  • Any written request for recorded information, e.g. email, fax or letter
  • Must describe the required information
  • Must provide a name and contact details, e.g. email address
  • Can be from anyone, anywhere in the world
  • There is no need to mention FOI Act
  • A request should be taken on face value and the reasons behind the request should not affect our response (motive and applicant blind)

If you get something which you think could be an FOI request forward it to foi@gloucestershire.gov.uk or phone 01452 425071 or 01452 42 5186.

Security breaches

So, what if something goes wrong? It is really important that you let somebody know. The council needs to control and fix the breach as well as ensuring that it doesn’t happen again, but we don’t know where the problems are if we don’t hear about them.

A breach happens when information is deliberately or accidentally lost, wrongly used or destroyed, and/or stolen.

Examples include:

  • Damage to equipment, facilities or utilities;
  • Loss or misplacement of media (e.g. disks, tapes, paper) containing personal data or PROTECT or RESTRICTED information;
  • Virus and malicious attack;
  • Unauthorised access or attempted unauthorised access to information or ICT resources.

Details of who to inform are on staffnet at http://staffnet.gloscc.gov.uk/infosecurity
